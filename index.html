What to Do When You're Stopped by Police

A police encounter can escalate in seconds. Knowing your rights before it happens is the difference between protecting yourself and unknowingly forfeiting your rights. This guide covers exactly what to do — and what not to do — in the moment.

  1. Stay calm. You have the right to remain silent — invoke it clearly: "I am invoking my right to remain silent."
  2. You can refuse a search. Say: "I do not consent to a search." This matters even if they search anyway.
  3. Ask if you are free to go. If not detained, you may leave. If detained, ask why.
  4. Do not physically resist, even if the stop is unlawful. Fight it in court, not on the street.
  5. Request an attorney immediately if arrested: "I want a lawyer." Then stop talking.
  6. Document everything you can remember as soon as it's safe — witnesses, badge numbers, time, location.

Policy Watch

Bills & Policies Affecting Black America.

Laws are made while most people aren't watching. We watch so you can act.

John R. Lewis Voting Rights Advancement Act

Active

Would restore and strengthen the Voting Rights Act of 1965 by requiring federal preclearance for voting law changes in jurisdictions with a history of discrimination.

🏛 Federal

George Floyd Justice in Policing Act

At Risk

Targets police misconduct by banning chokeholds, limiting qualified immunity, and creating a national police misconduct registry. Stalled in the Senate.

🏛 Federal

Fair Chance Housing Ordinances

Passed

City-level laws limiting landlords from using criminal history to deny housing. Now active in Seattle, Denver, and several other major cities.

🗺 State/Local

CROWN Act (Create a Respectful and Open World for Natural Hair)

Pending

Prohibits discrimination based on hair texture and protective hairstyles. Passed in 24 states; pending federal passage.
